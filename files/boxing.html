BOXING!

Overview of Boxing

Boxing is a combat sport and martial art where two participants exchange punches in a ring using protective gloves, hand wraps, and mouthguards. Matches are divided into timed rounds and supervised by a referee. Winners are decided by knockout, stoppage, disqualification, resignation, or judges' scores.

Types of Boxing

"Boxing" typically refers to Western boxing, but it also includes striking sports like kickboxing, Muay Thai, Lethwei, savate, and sanda, which incorporate kicks, elbows, knees, and headbutts depending on the rules.

Historical Origins

Boxing dates back to prehistoric times, with evidence from Egyptian and Sumerian carvings in the third millennium BCE. It became an Olympic event in Ancient Greece in 688 BCE and developed further in 19th-century Britain with the Marquess of Queensberry Rules.

Modern Boxing

Boxing exists in amateur and professional formats. Amateur boxing, seen in the Olympics and international games, focuses on point scoring and safety. Professional boxing features longer bouts, with winners determined by judges if no knockout or stoppage occurs.

Broader Influence

Boxing techniques have influenced martial arts, military systems, and other combat sports, solidifying its role as a prominent sport and method of self-defense globally.

History


The earliest visual evidence of any type of boxing comes from Egypt and Sumer both from the third millennium BC.[6][7] A relief sculpture from Egyptian Thebes (c. 1350 BC) shows both boxers and spectators.[7] These early Middle-Eastern and Egyptian depictions showed contests where fighters were either bare-fisted or had a band supporting the wrist. The earliest evidence of use of gloves can be found in Minoan Crete.

Rules


The Marquess of Queensberry Rules

Established in 1867, these rules form the foundation of modern boxing. Matches consist of 9 to 12 three-minute rounds with one-minute breaks where fighters rest in their corners and receive coaching and care.

Match Conduct

  • A referee oversees the fight, ensuring safety, ruling on fouls, and counting knocked-down fighters.

  • Up to three ringside judges score the bout based on punches, defense, knockdowns, and other criteria, which can lead to controversial decisions.

  • Fighters return to their corners at the end of each round, and the match is decided by score if it "goes the distance."

Winning the Match

  • A fighter may win by knockout (KO) if their opponent is unable to rise after a 10-count following a legal punch.

  • A technical knockout (TKO) occurs when the referee, doctor, or corner determines a fighter cannot safely continue.

  • Decisions can also be unanimous, split, or result in a draw if the match reaches the end of the scheduled rounds.

Special Rules
  • Standing Eight Count:

    The referee may pause the fight and count to eight if they believe a fighter is in danger, treating it as a knockdown for scoring purposes

Equipment

Since boxing involves forceful, repetitive punching, precautions must be taken to prevent damage to bones in the hand. Most trainers do not allow boxers to train and spar without wrist wraps and boxing gloves. Hand wraps are used to secure the bones in the hand, and the gloves are used to protect the hands from blunt injury, allowing boxers to throw punches with more force than if they did not use them.
